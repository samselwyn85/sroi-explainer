A plain-English guide with a working tool

How a Social Return on Investment model actually works

SROI answers one question: for every dollar put into a programme, how much social value comes out? This guide walks through a real, published New Zealand example step by step — then hands you a calculator so you can build your own.

Where this comes from

A philosophy and a machine

This guide is built from two published New Zealand reports that work as a pair.

The philosophy

Integrating Māori Worldviews with Health Economic Evaluation · Manatū Hauora · 2025

Asks what "value" means from a te ao Māori position, and which Western economic tools can carry that meaning without crushing it. Its answer: run two parallel streams — price what can honestly be priced, and carry everything else whole. Never force the two into one number.

The machine

VOYCE – Whakarongo Mai Social Impact Assessment · Matatihi · 2026

That philosophy actually executed on a real organisation — a national advocacy service for care-experienced tamariki and rangatahi. Nine benefits priced through the Treasury's CBAx conventions, producing a headline result of $21.85m of social value from $5.64m of cost: a ratio of 3.9 : 1.

The relationship between them matters more than either one. The machine is only credible because the philosophy decided in advance what would never be turned into dollars.

The one idea to hold onto

Every SROI is a decision about where to put the fence

On one side: outcomes you convert to dollars. On the other: outcomes you name, evidence, and report — but deliberately refuse to price. The worked example puts the fence here:

$21.85m ÷ $5.64m = 3.9 : 1
Present value of benefits over three years, divided by programme cost.

Held whole

Named and evidenced — and deliberately never given a dollar figure.

  • Cultural identity & whakapapa
  • Restoration of mana & dignity
  • Whānau relationships repaired
  • Trust and sense of safety
  • Leadership & governance capability
  • Collective voice & system change

These aren't leftovers. Pricing them was judged the wrong operation — it would flatten a taonga into a proxy. They travel to the decision-maker in a parallel stream, carried by the Treasury's He Ara Waiora wellbeing framework, and are never summed with the dollar figures.

Before the maths

Three ideas that make the arithmetic make sense

1 · "Value" is a spectrum — price is only its far edge

Value runs from ethics (what we ought to hold dear) to price (what it goes for). Western economics collapsed toward the price end; te ao Māori sits naturally at the ethics end — while always having used exchange value too. Money here is just a symbol, like the word "maunga" standing in for a mountain: a convenient representation, never the thing itself. A price signals importance to a system that runs on prices. It doesn't claim to be the value.

2 · Health and wellbeing sit in three concentric levels

The report fuses Durie's Te Whare Tapa Whā with Hēnare's Economy of Mana into three rings: individual (physical, mental), social (kinship, economic), and cosmological (spiritual, ecological) — bound together by tauutuutu, reciprocal exchange that must leave both sides better off. The surprising claim: the social ring is the most load-bearing, because it facilitates the individual and mediates the cosmological.

3 · Dimensional preservation — the rule that builds the fence

Value exists in spiritual, social, clinical and economic dimensions at the same time, and these are complementary, not substitutable. You don't trade one for another, and you don't add them up. So the architecture is two parallel streams that both reach the decision-maker's desk and are never merged. Keep this rule in mind when you use the calculator: the tool only ever computes the priced stream. The other stream is words, and stays words.

The machine, end to end

Eight steps from theory to ratio

This is the whole method as the worked example runs it. Steps 3 and 4 are where all the judgement lives — everything after them is arithmetic.

  1. Write the Theory of Change and the counterfactual

    What does the programme do, for whom, through what mechanism — and what would the world look like without it? Every benefit you price later must trace back to a mechanism named here.

  2. Define the cohort

    Unique, de-duplicated people. The worked example uses 2,453 tamariki and rangatahi. If the same person appears twice, everything downstream is inflated.

  3. Choose and fence the benefits

    Pick benefits that are mutually exclusive — one causal pathway each, no overlaps. Decide what stays unpriced and why. This step is the fence.

    → Parallel stream branches off here (and never merges back) The outcomes held whole — identity, mana, trust, whānau repair, leadership, collective voice — are reported through He Ara Waiora as evidenced narrative. They reach the decision-maker intact, alongside the ratio, never inside it.

  4. Parameterise each benefit, per year

    Three numbers per benefit per year: segment share (what % of the cohort is plausibly exposed to this pathway, above business-as-usual), success rate (what % of those achieve the change, net of what would have happened anyway), and units per person (how much change — a year of improved belonging, seven nights of housing, a quarter-year of benefit avoided).

  5. Attach a dollar proxy to each unit

    Treasury's CBAx database first; peer-reviewed literature where CBAx has no match. A year of improved belonging: $3,455. An ED presentation avoided: $571. A year of Jobseeker Support: $17,168.

  6. Discount everything to present value

    Future benefits are worth slightly less than today's. The example uses the Treasury default of 2% real, applied mid-year, over a three-year horizon.

  7. Divide by cost

    Present value of benefits ÷ present value of costs = the benefit–cost ratio. The example: $21.85m ÷ $5.64m = 3.9:1. Subtract instead of divide and you get the net present value: +$16.2m.

  8. Stress-test it

    Rerun the model under optimistic, pessimistic, and funding-cut assumptions. The example's ratio holds between 2.9:1 and 4.3:1 across all scenarios — that range is what makes the headline believable.

    Side-car: the economic multiplier Some reports add an input–output analysis ($7.13m of spend supports ~$9.65m of GDP and ~111 jobs). That's a footprint — how money circulates — not impact, and it never enters the ratio.

Design note: there is no separate "attribution %" in this method

Classic SROI tutorials teach four discount multipliers — deadweight, displacement, attribution, drop-off. This method embeds all of them inside the three parameters instead: segment share only counts people exposed above business-as-usual, success rate is already net of what would have happened anyway, and the year-by-year taper is the drop-off. Cleaner arithmetic, no double-adjusting — but it means the attribution judgement can't be audited separately from the reach judgement. Know which convention you're using before you compare models.

The engine

The formula is one line. Everything else is judgement.

Nine benefits × three years × three parameters = 81 numbers, plus nine proxies. That's the entire model. It fits on a napkin — which is exactly why the judgement behind each number matters so much.

# for each benefit, for each year t = 1, 2, 3
quantity = cohort × segment_share × success_rate × units_per_person
value = quantity × proxy_$
pv = value ÷ (1 + 0.02) ^ (t − 0.5) ← mid-year discounting
 
PV_benefits = sum of every pv → $21.85m in the example
BCR = PV_benefits ÷ PV_costs → 3.9 : 1
NPV = PV_benefits − PV_costs → +$16.2m

What the numbers actually say: 95% of the value is two lines

Life satisfaction ($11.38m) plus belonging ($9.47m) make up $20.85m of the $21.85m total. The other seven benefits — every avoided-cost and fiscal-saving line — add under $1m combined. Delete them all in the calculator and the ratio only falls from 3.9 to 3.7.

That's not a flaw — Treasury supplies wellbeing proxies precisely so that wellbeing can count. But it tells you where the evidential weight sits: this kind of model is a wellbeing story resting on survey evidence, with fiscal savings as supporting detail. Build and defend the two big lines accordingly.

The other side of the fence

Two different reasons not to price something

"Not monetised" covers two completely different decisions. Conflating them is the most common way a replica of this method goes wrong.

Pricing it would be the wrong operation

Some outcomes are intrinsic ends, not inputs to something else. Cultural identity isn't valuable because it improves school attendance; it's valuable because it's valuable. Reduce it to a proxy and you've made a category error.

These can never cross the fence, no matter how good the data gets. The kaupapa decides, not the evidence.

Pricing it was possible but reckless

Other outcomes could be monetised — proxies exist — but were left out to stay conservative: they'd overlap a line already priced (double counting), or the causal chain is too long to defend.

These could cross the fence later if the evidence base strengthens. That's an evidence position, not a values one.

If you ever build this as a real system: use three states, not two

Every outcome should carry a flag — PRICED, HELD_INTRINSIC, or EXCLUDED_PENDING_EVIDENCE. Build it as a yes/no and within a year someone under deadline pressure will quietly promote a taonga outcome into a dollar figure because a proxy turned up. The three-state flag is a governance control, not metadata.

Beyond the calculator

The model is easy. The data spine is the job.

81 parameters and 9 proxies is a config file — the whole ratio computes in a few lines of code, and the calculator on the next page proves it. What's hard is the denominator of every fraction: a de-duplicated person record with exposure flags (was this person reachable by this pathway?) and outcome flags (did the change happen?), tracked over time.

Without that spine, every segment share and success rate is an educated guess dressed as a parameter. With it, they become counts. And the one artefact worth building that most published SROIs don't have: a single parameter register — every judgement in one governed table, each with a source, an owner, a date, and its three-state fence flag. The fence isn't a diagram; it's a constraint you enforce in the data, or it doesn't exist.
